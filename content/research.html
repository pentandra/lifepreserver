What is Research?

That may sound like a silly question. But sometimes we are so busy with research that we don't take the time to think about what research is. Research is more than a publication.

Everything that you do to go from a question to an answer. Not only do you want to share your answer, but you want to share how you got there---your journey of discovery. Don't leave out the middle. meat. more rich Research is the process of discovery. A discovery of questions, of clues, and of ideas that grow together until answers are revealed. It is a cycle---a quest to understand self and the world around us. When you discover something new about the world, it doesn't just add to the world's knowledgebase, it changes you. A discovery is exhilarating. All you want to do is share it with others.

Research has many forms in many fields, including science, engineering, history, anthropology, humanities, among others. In each of these domains, research is a process involving questions, hypotheses, study, and experimentation, arriving eventually at a provable conclusion. This process is not always linear, but oftentimes follows a seemingly random and cyclical pattern. One question often leads to more questions. and that they're all connected. This process continues until answers are found and questions are laid to rest.

Research has been around for centuries. While new tools have come into play, the research process itself hasn't changed a whole lot. Many in the research community are calling for improvements in both the quality of the research and efficiency of the process.

While the research process across fields may seem very similar on the surface, differences in research communities, knowledge representations, and vocabularies arise under closer scrutiny. We believe that to be most effective, research software needs to be designed specifically for the field it is trying to support. We have discovered three elements that are essential in creating modern, domain-specific research systems: clarity, context, and collaboration.

Clarity

See what pieces you used and how you used it.

Research has to be understandable to be useful. Let's compare research to a puzzle. Unlike a puzzle that you buy from the store, this puzzle box is unlabeled. You know that it makes a picture, but you don't know what that picture is. After you put the pieces together, someone else looking at what you did should be able to see the same thing you do, whether you have put the puzzle together correctly or not.

Clarity in research is being able to see the research from the original researcher's point of view. Research is more than conclusions; therefore, all of the pieces of information that were used to come to a conclusion need to be accessible and transparent. In addition, research must clearly show how the data was used to answer a question. Otherwise, the researchers that come after will not know how you put the pieces together.

Context

Seeing the big picture. What does this mean? How the picture fits into the greater body of knowledge, subject, all of humanity the world, or of an individual life. There are many contexts to consider. People may be working on similar things in different specializations that need to step back and see past the compartments. life context, research context. How does this affect me and the world? How does the world affect my research, and how does my research affect the world? Individual pieces of a puzzle have no meaning until they are put in context. You don't pick one puzzle piece out of the box and expect to see the whole picture. As you add pieces, however, a picture begins to emerge and you can see the true meaning of each piece.

Research is only correctly understood in its proper context. This means studying not only the subject of the research, but also how it relates to the world around it. When that fuller context is considered, new avenues for research are opened and barriers are breached. Holes in the research, if any, are also revealed.
