What is Research?

Research is a journey of discovery. It is a process---the ultimate goal of which is to understand ourselves and the world around us. By nature, we are each inquisitive. We want to know how things work. We have questions that beg for answers. Whenever we look for answers we are doing research, though we may not think of it as such. In a sense, all of us are researchers, even if we don't realize it!

Research is found in many fields, including science, engineering, history, anthropology, and humanities. Research is even found in our own homes when we're trying to decide which TV to buy. No matter where it is found, research is a process involving questions, hypotheses, study, and experimentation, arriving eventually at a provable conclusion.

A hypothesis is an unverified answer to a research question.

Hypothesis on Wikipedia

While we may come to a conclusion about which TV to buy using very informal research, some situations require a more formal approach. Formal research is just another way of saying credible research. Formal research contains features that help others believe the research conclusions, which usually involve opinion and need to be backed up with evidence.

Traditionally, universities and other large research organizations have contributed the vast majority of research to the world. While these organizations will continue to play a large role in the research of the future, we believe that modern research and e-research systems will lower the entry barrier for quality research contributions, making it possible for more people to contribute to high-quality research projects.

The Research Process

So, how do you learn how to research? In order to answer that question we need to take a closer look at the research process. Research is messy. To an outside observer, research may even look a bit like chaos theory. Even so, a good researcher is fully aware of the research process and knows exactly what is going on and why.

Let's take a journey through the research process. First, we'll take a drive through the inputs and outputs. Then, we'll stop by the phases of the research process. After that, we'll define the fundamental unit of research. And finally we'll visit the pillars of proof. While the research process can vary a lot depending on the field of research, this journey should at least give you a good intro to the fundamentals.

Before we can start our journey, however, we need a question. Questions are always the beginning of the research process. Conclusions are the end. All of the good stuff---the journey of discovery---is sandwiched in-between. Only after we have a question can we try to find an answer!

Well, what are you waiting for? Let's go!

Inputs and Outputs: The "What's" of Research

The easiest things to grasp when talking about the research process are the tangible things--the things that we can see, touch, read and write. These are the things we are left with after completing some phase of the research process.

In research, one thing flows into another. As Figure 2 shows, an output from one phase is the input for another. These inputs and outputs do not follow some predetermined path, but rather are dependent on the of the previous phase of the research process.

Data

Raw, uninterpreted stuff. Given a field of research, data is the source from which researchers in that field gather information.

Could include measurements made during experiments by a physicist or historical documents for a genealogist or historian. This can even include research papers that are studied during the course of research.

Information
This is interpreted data. It should be understood by someone that is familiar with the research domain.
Evidence
How the information pertains to the research question.

Phases: The "How's" of Research

Research is a lot like chess. Strategy is required to assemble the bits of information the right way.

  1. Orientation--assessing the situation and orienting to the possibilities
  2. Exploration--Trying out some of the possibilities
  3. Investigation--Digging deeper and choosing the best possibility
  4. Proof--Confirming that the choice is right

Oftentimes, we can cycle through these phases in a matter of seconds. Sometimes it takes longer. It depends on the problem at hand.

While chess has a very finite number of possibilities, research is dynamic with almost limitless possibilities. Unlike chess, you discover the rules and the playing pieces as you go along. However, just like chess, your eventual placement in the game is the sum of all the previous moves you have made on the game board.

These inputs and outputs point the way in research like a compass points the way through an overgrown forest.

A Unit of Research

Like scientists that break matter down into atoms, we need to isolate a unit of research so that research can be more manageable.

To make these in's and out's more manageable we need a few more tools. Large research problems can be overwhelming. If we break up these large problems into bite-sized pieces, research becomes more straightforward. Likewise, as we look backwards and evaluate our own research, we need a measuring stick to measure the quality of what we have accomplished.

A case is the fundamental unit of research.

In our software systems we call this whole thing a case. A case is made of a question, its conclusion (if one has been reached), and all of the research and analysis undertaken to answer that question. Think of it as a little bundle of research.

The research process is not always linear, but oftentimes follows a seemingly random and cyclical pattern. One question often leads to more questions. This process continues until answers are found and questions are laid to rest.

The Five Pillars of Proof

So after we have done some research, how do we know how good it is? We know by looking back and comparing what we have done to a standard. The standard varies depending on the domain, but generally includes the following checkpoints:

Attribution
Don't be a weasel. Did I give credit where credit is due?
Comprehensive
Research should be thorough. Did I cover all my bases?
Well-Reasoned
Think about it. Does the conclusion make sense logically?
Verifiable
Can someone else retrace my steps? Will they end up at the same place I did?
Transparent
Don't hide your light under a bushel. Explain what you mean so that someone else can understand it.

An inward perspective is also needed in research. We look back and evaluate our progress, making sure that we have not strayed from the path and that we have not left any stone unturned. Peer review gives us a second opinion as others look back on our research to assess its quality.

All research conclusions involve opinion. It is up to you, as a researcher, to prove that your opinion is right.

The Future of Research: A Manifesto

In 1945, the Director of the US Office of Scientific Research and Development said:

This situation has not changed a whole lot since then. Yes, we have computers and digital documents now, but we are still stuck in a paper publishing paradigm. It can still take one or two years to get a research paper published! And then, we still only publish our research conclusions. While conclusions are important, research is more than a conclusion. Most of our research process is not being published and is being lost. This makes it harder to build on each other's research.

Why is our sharing process still so limited? Why do we limit scholarly papers to the question and answer, when sharing the entire journey of discovery would be more beneficial for everyone? Research is a rich experience that should be shareable.

While the research process across fields may seem very similar on the surface, differences in research communities, knowledge representations, and vocabularies arise under closer scrutiny. We believe that to be most effective, research software needs to be designed specifically for the field it is trying to support. However, we think that the following three elements should be present in any modern research system:

  1. Clarity
  2. Context
  3. Collaboration

Clarity

Like tesserae in a mosaic, the building blocks of research should be visible and accessible so others can understand how you put the pieces together.

Research should be clear and transparent. All of the pieces of information that contributed to the conclusion should be accessible. We should be able to see the research from the original researcher's point of view. Research must clearly show not only what data was used, but how the data was used to answer the question. Otherwise, the researchers that come after will not know how you put the pieces together.

Clarity is important not only so that others can understand and verify your research conclusion, but because you don't want to leave out your journey of discovery. This is the "meat" of research. Why are we leaving out the good stuff?

Context

Individual tesserae have no meaning until they are put into context. Only as you look at the bigger picture do you see the true meaning of each piece.

Our world grows increasingly compartmentalized. We pursue our specialization as if with blinders, trying to push forward progress in our chosen field. Modern researchers and research systems need to take a more holistic approach. Systems thinkers need to show how research in one field relates to research in another. We need bridge builders that can see the big picture and can make connections between bodies of knowledge, subjects, and individual lives.

Research is only correctly understood in its proper context. When that fuller context is considered, new avenues for research are opened and barriers are breached. Holes in the research, if any, are also revealed.

Collaboration

Clarity and context are prerequisites to true research collaboration.

Modern research and research systems are collaborative. The rewards of research collaboration outweigh any of the benefits of traditional reward systems. Life is too short to be stuck in traditional research silos.

There is still so much to learn about ourselves and our world. Because good research naturally proliferates, there is no danger of running out of work to do. However, we will move faster and build better research as we work together.
