What is Research?

Research is a process of discovery. A discovery of questions, of clues, and of ideas that grow together until answers are revealed. It is a cycle---a quest to understand self and the world around us. When you discover something new about the world, it doesn't just add to the world's knowledgebase, it changes you. It is exhilarating and you can't wait to share it.

Research has many forms in many fields, including science, engineering, history, anthropology, humanities, among others. No matter what the field, research is always a process involving questions, study, hypotheses, and experimentation, arriving eventually at a conclusion. This process is not always linear, but oftentimes follows a seemingly random and cyclical pattern. One question often leads to more questions. This continues until answers are found and questions are laid to rest.

Essential Elements of Modern Research

The research process has been around for centuries. While new tools have come into play, the process itself hasn't changed much. Many in the research community are calling for improvements in both the quality of the research and efficiency of the process.

Clarity

Research has to be understandable to be useful. Let's compare research to a puzzle. Unlike a puzzle that you buy from the store, this puzzle box is unlabeled. You know that it makes a picture, but you don't know what that picture is. After you put the pieces together, someone else looking at what you did should be able to see the same thing you do, whether you have put the puzzle together correctly or not.

Clarity in research is being able to see the research from the original researcher's point of view. Research is more than conclusions; therefore, all of the pieces of information that were used to come to a conclusion need to be accessible and transparent. In addition, research must clearly show how the information was used to answer a question. Otherwise, that information is effectually lost to researchers that come after.

Context

Individual pieces of a puzzle have no meaning until they are put in context. You don't pick one puzzle piece out of the box and expect to see the whole picture. As you add pieces, however, a picture begins to emerge and you can see the true meaning of each piece.

Research is only correctly understood in its proper context. This means studying not only the subject of the research, but also how it relates to the world around it. When that fuller context is considered, new avenues for research are opened and brick walls are surmounted. Holes in the research are also revealed.

Collaboration

Putting a complicated puzzle together with friends is much quicker and more enjoyable than tackling it alone.

None of us is as smart as all of us. Sharing just one piece, even the conclusion, is not enough. This gives peer review a new meaning. This is called Open Research.
