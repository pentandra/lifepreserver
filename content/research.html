What is Research?

That may sound like a silly question, but have you ever considered what research is? It could be that research can seem so intuitive. By nature, we are inquisitive. We want answers to our questions. Every time we try to find an answer we are doing research. We just may not think of it as such. All of us are researchers, even if we don't realize it!

So what is research, then?

Research is the process of discovery. It is a cycle---a quest to understand ourselves and the world around us. Research is found in many fields, including science, engineering, history, anthropology, humanities. It is even found in our own homes when we're trying to decide which TV to buy. In each of these domains, research is a process involving questions, hypotheses, study, and experimentation, arriving eventually at a provable conclusion.

The Research Process

Research is an outward expansion of knowledge and understanding. When we discover something new, we feel enlightened. We want to share our new understanding with the world. If you have ever discovered something new, you know what I mean.

An inward perspective is also needed in research. We look back and evaluate our progress, making sure that we have not strayed from the path and that we have not left any stone unturned. Peer review gives us a second opinion as others look back on our research to assess its quality.

To make these in's and out's more manageable we need a few more tools. Large research problems can be overwhelming. If we break up these large problems into bite-sized pieces, research becomes more straightforward. Likewise, as we look backwards and evaluate our own research, we need a measuring stick to measure the quality of what we have accomplished.

A Unit of Research

So how do we break these large research problems into smaller pieces? What is research made of? Like scientists that break down matter into atoms, we need to find the fundamental unit of research. We know that questions and answers are the beginning and end of the research process, so a question and an answer frame a unit of research. All of the good stuff, your journey of discovery, is sandwiched in between. We call the whole thing, both frame and filling, a case. Whenever we have a question, we start a case. This case keeps track of everything we have done to find an answer to that question. It is a little bundle of research.

A Vision of Modern Research

In 1945, the Director of the US Office of Scientific Research and Development said:

This situation has not changed a whole lot since then. Yes, we have computers and digital documents now, but we are still stuck in a paper publishing paradigm. It can still take one or two years to get a research paper published! And then, we still only publish our research conclusions. While conclusions are important, research is more than a conclusion. Much of our research process is not being published and is being lost. This makes it harder to build on each other's research.

Why is our sharing process still so limited? Why do we limit scholarly papers to the question and answer, when sharing the entire journey of discovery would be more beneficial for everyone? Research is a rich experience that should be shareable.

While the research process across fields may seem very similar on the surface, differences in research communities, knowledge representations, and vocabularies arise under closer scrutiny. We believe that to be most effective, research software needs to be designed specifically for the field it is trying to support. However, we think that the following three elements should be present in any modern, domain-specific research system:

  1. Clarity
  2. Context
  3. Collaboration

Clarity

See what pieces you used and how you used it.

Research has to be understandable to be useful. Let's compare research to a puzzle. Unlike a puzzle that you buy from the store, this puzzle box is unlabeled. You know that it makes a picture, but you don't know what that picture is. After you put the pieces together, someone else looking at what you did should be able to see the same thing you do, whether you have put the puzzle together correctly or not.

Clarity in research is being able to see the research from the original researcher's point of view. Research is more than conclusions; therefore, all of the pieces of information that were used to come to a conclusion need to be accessible and transparent. In addition, research must clearly show how the data was used to answer a question. Otherwise, the researchers that come after will not know how you put the pieces together.

Individual pieces of a puzzle have no meaning until they are put in context. You don't pick one puzzle piece out of the box and expect to see the whole picture. As you add pieces, however, a picture begins to emerge and you can see the true meaning of each piece.

Research is only correctly understood in its proper context. This means studying not only the subject of the research, but also how it relates to the world around it. When that fuller context is considered, new avenues for research are opened and barriers are breached. Holes in the research, if any, are also revealed.
