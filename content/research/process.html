Research 101

The Research Process

So, how do you learn how to research? In order to answer that question we need to take a closer look at the research process. Research is messy. To an outside observer, research may even look a bit like chaos theory. Even so, a good researcher is fully aware of the research process and knows exactly what is going on and why.

Let's take a journey through the research process. First, we'll take a drive through the inputs and outputs. Then, we'll stop by the phases of the research process. After that, we'll define the fundamental unit of research. And finally we'll visit the pillars of proof. While the research process can vary a lot depending on the field of research, this journey should at least give you a good intro to the fundamentals.

Before we can start our journey, however, we need a question. Questions are always the beginning of the research process. Conclusions are the end. All of the good stuff---the journey of discovery---is sandwiched in-between. Only after we have a question can we try to find an answer!

Well, what are you waiting for? Let's go!

Inputs and Outputs: The "What's" of Research

The easiest things to grasp when talking about the research process are the tangible things--the things that we can see, touch, read and write. After completing a phase of the research process we are left with these tangibles, which we call research outputs.

In research, everything is connected. One thing flows into another. As Figure 2 shows, an output from one phase can be recycled as the input for another. Inputs are dependent upon the outputs of previous phases of the research process. They do not follow a predetermined path.

Between the question and conclusion, the three other parts form the skeletal scaffolding of the research process:

Data

Data is raw, uninterpreted stuff. Given a field of research, data is the source from which researchers in that field gather information.

Could include measurements made during experiments by a physicist or historical documents for a genealogist or historian. This can even include research papers that are studied during the course of research.

Information

Information is interpreted data---interpreted as knowledge of the research domain. It should be understood by someone that is familiar with the research domain.

Evidence

Evidence is interpreted information---interpreted in the context of the question. How does this information answer the question?

Outputs beckon to us, prodding us onward, wanting to be selected for the next phase of the research process. Inputs point the way to go like a guide points the way through an overgrown forest.

We, as researchers, ultimately choose the course to follow. Choice is the fundamental navigator of the research process. Choose carefully.

Phases: The "How's" of Research

Research is a lot like chess. Strategy is required to assemble the bits of information the right way.

  1. Orientation--assessing the situation and orienting to the possibilities
  2. Exploration--Trying out some of the possibilities
  3. Investigation--Digging deeper and choosing the best possibility
  4. Proof--Confirming that the choice is right

Oftentimes, we can cycle through these phases in a matter of seconds. Sometimes it takes longer. It depends on the problem at hand.

While chess has a very finite number of possibilities, research is dynamic with almost limitless possibilities. Unlike chess, you discover the rules and the playing pieces as you go along. However, just like chess, your eventual placement in the game is the sum of all the previous moves you have made on the game board.

These inputs and outputs point the way in research like a compass points the way through an overgrown forest.

A Unit of Research

Like scientists that break matter down into atoms, we need to isolate a unit of research so that research can be more manageable.

To make these in's and out's more manageable we need a few more tools. Large research problems can be overwhelming. If we break up these large problems into bite-sized pieces, research becomes more straightforward. Likewise, as we look backwards and evaluate our own research, we need a measuring stick to measure the quality of what we have accomplished.

A case is the fundamental unit of research.

In our software systems we call this whole thing a case. A case is made of a question, its conclusion (if one has been reached), and all of the research and analysis undertaken to answer that question. Think of it as a little bundle of research.

The research process is not always linear, but oftentimes follows a seemingly random and cyclical pattern. One question often leads to more questions. This process continues until answers are found and questions are laid to rest.

As a company, we are focused on the building blocks of research.

The Five Pillars of Proof

So after we have done some research, how do we know how good it is? We know by looking back and comparing what we have done to a standard. The standard varies depending on the domain, but generally includes the following checkpoints:

Attribution
Don't be a weasel. Did I give credit where credit is due?
Comprehensive
Research should be thorough. Did I cover all my bases?
Well-Reasoned
Think about it. Does the conclusion make sense logically?
Verifiable
Can someone else retrace my steps? Will they end up at the same place I did?
Transparent
Don't hide your light under a bushel. Explain what you mean so that someone else can understand it.

An inward perspective is also needed in research. We look back and evaluate our progress, making sure that we have not strayed from the path and that we have not left any stone unturned. Peer review gives us a second opinion as others look back on our research to assess its quality.

All research conclusions involve opinion. It is up to you, as a researcher, to prove that your opinion is right.
