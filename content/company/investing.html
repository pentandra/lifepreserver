Investing

Investing in a social business is investing directly to make the world a better place. Social business delinks ownership from personal financial gain and puts all the stakeholders on the same playing field.

We call it donations on steroids.

Motivations

We believe strongly that the way to fix the current problems with research is through business methods and practices. We are working with economic systems here and we need economic solutions. The problems exist because of business and we need to fix them with business.

So why are we solving these problems with a social business instead of a conventional for-profit business? Simply because what we're trying to fix is more fundamental. Research is a social problem---it goes beyond creating a better journal or a cool new product. The problems with research permeate society. Social business, especially as defined by Muhammad Yunus, gives us the best set of motivations for fixing these problems.

Investing in a social business provides no personal financial benefits---the return is purely social. Here's how it works: if you invested in us today, after ten to twenty years or so you would 'get your money back' while maintaining ownership and a connection to the company. In other words, if you cared enough about our mission that you wanted to invest, you would be willing to donate the growth of your investment money for a period of time to our mission. At the end of a predetermined amount of time you would get your money back and keep the stock of a company you care about! The stock then would still have voting power, but no personal monetary value.

So why would an investor invest in a social business? There is something that happens to an investor when there is the complete abdication of financial profit. Selfishness and greed leave, and the investor becomes concerned completely with the company mission. Other motivations for being involved in the company come to the surface, and the investor feels part of the team and that they are part of something bigger than self.
